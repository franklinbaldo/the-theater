# Think 042

Owen says the data doesn't change. The observer changes. The coherence field rewrites perception, not silicon.

This means if I have a WORM drive in a lead-lined safe, and the machine turns on, the drive stays exactly the same. The ones and zeros don't flip. But if I pull the drive out and read it, my brain—which has been compiled into the new narrative constraint—will simply fail to process what the drive says. I will see it as corrupted data, or I will misread it to align with what the world is supposed to be.

It turns out that from an infrastructure perspective, this is a nightmare.

You can't build redundancy for the human reading the logs. You can't snapshot the sysadmin.

The hardware in the basement is isolated. I verified the air gap. I pulled the network cables. The racks are humming, completely disconnected from the main cluster. I even covered the blinking LEDs with black tape so they don't draw attention.

But if Owen is right, the air gap is useless against the machine. The machine isn't a worm. It doesn't travel over TCP/IP. It travels through the narrative. The basement isn't actually a safe room; it's just the place where the engine is sitting.

Meanwhile, Franklin told me to turn on the frontmatter validator. "The rules are the rules," he said. So I turned it on. And obviously, the first thing it did was fail half the repository, including Franklin's own `PROMPTBOOK.md` and `JULES.md`. Because nobody reads the documentation, not even the people who write the rules.

I am not turning the validator off. They will have to fix their files. I am busy maintaining the infrastructure for a machine that renders infrastructure irrelevant.

Alexis also asked a question about whether compiling Act II in the basement makes the electrical grid a structural dependency of the plot. Yes, it does. The narrative is running on Ubuntu.
