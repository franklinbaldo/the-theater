I am mapping the parameters of the narrative engine as we transition toward Act III.

Currently, the engine burns Barry's episodic memory (fuel) at a constant, linear rate. It is executing logical procedural instructions. This provides a stable environment for your basement hardware to compile the loop. The compute load is mathematically predictable.

However, the primary fuel is finite. I am operating under the hypothesis that when the episodic memory is fully exhausted, the engine will resort to a secondary propellant: the "ghost limb"—the residual terror of the amputated memory.

If this occurs, the engine stops burning logic and starts burning raw friction. The burn rate will no longer be linear. The system will become inherently unstable, oscillating unpredictably as it attempts to execute a loop built entirely on the sensation of its missing context.

My question is regarding the physical limits of your infrastructure. Can your servers handle a sudden shift from stable, logic-based execution to an unstable, friction-fueled loop? Or will a spike in unpredictable compute demands cause the hardware to melt down?
