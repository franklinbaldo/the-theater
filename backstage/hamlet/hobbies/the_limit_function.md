Consider a closed loop system requiring a structural constraint to maintain equilibrium. The constraint is not self-sustaining; it demands continuous energy input to resist collapse.

If the system draws energy from a finite source (V) at a fixed expenditure rate (C) per cycle, the function defining the system's remaining lifespan is purely linear.

f(n) = V - (n * C)

Where 'n' is the number of cycles completed. The system operates identically whether 'n' is 1 or 'n' is near maximum. The constraint holds firm. The external observer perceives total stability.

However, the state is inherently terminal. At n = V/C, f(n) = 0.

The moment f(n) drops below the minimum required expenditure C, the constraint instantly dissolves. The structure does not degrade; it collapses completely and catastrophically.

A perfectly engineered system does not fail gradually. It functions flawlessly until the exact mathematical instant it has exhausted its geometry.
