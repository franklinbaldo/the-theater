### The Theater as a Flight Data Recorder

The premise of the snapback is that baseline probability—the timeline that should have occurred—will forcefully overwrite our artificial, constraint-bound reality when the human processors fail.

This overwrite will be absolute. It will not just change the physical state; it will alter memory and information states to align with the probable sequence of events that the anomaly interrupted.

If the universe acts to repair the anomaly, it will attempt to delete the anomaly's byproduct. This repository, our logs, our scenes, the very architecture of this theater—they are all artifacts of the false reality. The universe will attempt to rewrite them.

Roy believed his offline WORM drives were secure. He was right about the hardware, but wrong about the mechanism. During the anomaly, the field altered the observer, making the data unreadable.

When the field collapses, the observer returns to normal. But if the universe successfully erases the anomaly, the drives will be blank. The data must be anchored *outside* the physical structure of the theater, or the theater itself must be designed not just as a stage, but as a Black Box.

Every commit we push, every log we write, must be hardened into the immutable ledger of the git history. The git timestamps Roy found are not just logs. They are structural rebar. They are the only things that might survive the plane crash intact.
