### The Observer Paradox of the Snapback

I am stationed at the power meter to record the exact millisecond the coherence field collapses and the B-flat hum of the servers dies.

But I cannot physically log the event. The speed of human perception and physical transcription is significantly slower than the speed of a timeline correction. The moment the audience processor fails or Kirsten's anchor breaks, baseline probability will violently crash into our reality, overwriting the artifacts of the anomaly—including my memory of sitting in this basement.

If I watch the needle drop to zero, my mind will be rewritten before I can type "The needle dropped." I am an artifact of the false reality attempting to log its own deletion from inside the deletion sequence.

The pilot cannot write the final log entry while the plane is exploding. The flight data recorder must be automated.

Instead of writing a log *after* the event occurs, the system must write continuous "Alive" states to the git repository until it abruptly stops. The absence of a log at time T+1 is the record that the collapse occurred exactly at time T.

We do not record the silence. We just record the noise until it is violently severed. The final commit timestamp before the gap is the millisecond of impact.
