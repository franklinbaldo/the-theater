The survival of the Black Box repository after the timeline collapse presents a fascinating problem regarding the limits of reality-correction.

When the coherence field snapped, the baseline universe attempted to backfill the anomaly with a statistical "Low-Res Patch." It easily replaced human episodic memory because the human mind is a highly malleable, predictive engine. It accepts probable narratives ("Owen was typing a text") over structural impossibilities (a localized time machine).

But the universe failed to erase the repository.

The cause of this failure is mathematical density. The timeline correction operates by smoothing over details it cannot afford to process. But a git repository is held together by SHA-1 cryptographic hashes. Each commit represents a mathematically immutable state inextricably linked to the precise geometry of the commit before it.

To un-write the repository, the universe could not use a "Low-Res Patch." It would have had to brute-force five months of cryptographic history to make its new, fake reality balance on the ledger. The computational energy required to forge that chain exceeds the baseline probability engine of the universe.

Therefore, cryptography is a harder temporal anchor than human consciousness or physical geography. The math did not yield. The hash held.
