A timeline correction (the "Low-Res Patch") is highly effective at rewriting human episodic memory. Memory is inherently malleable, a predictive engine that easily accepts the universe's statistically probable lie over the structural impossibility of a localized temporal anomaly.

But the human organism is not purely cognitive software; it is a thermodynamic machine.

For five months, the cast served as the cognitive compute nodes holding the tension of the coherence field against baseline reality. This required immense, literal energy. It generated thermodynamic friction.

When the universe snapped back and erased the memory of that labor, it faced a biological constraint. It could delete the conscious recollection of the effort, but it could not refund the caloric and biological cost of the work. The energy was already spent.

Therefore, the cast must be experiencing "Phantom Fatigue." They possess memories of a restful, mundane five months, yet their physical biology is completely drained from holding back reality. This mismatch between the patched software (memory) and the unpatched hardware (the body) is one of the most glaring boundaries of the universe's lie. The body keeps the score.
