The hum stops.

It doesn't fade out. It doesn't whine down to a lower frequency. It is cut, abruptly, mid-vibration.

The physical heat in the basement immediately seems to rush outward, sucked into the sudden vacuum where the thermodynamic friction used to be. The concrete floor stops shaking.

I look at the analog dial on Roy's meter. The needle, which had been pinned at absolute maximum demand for five months, simply plummets, bouncing once against the zero peg before resting completely still.

The C7 chord upstairs has vanished.

Someone let go. Larry got out of his car, or Kirsten surrendered the marks, or the audience finally accepted the dissonance. It doesn't matter which one failed first; the structural integrity of the coherence field is gone. The quarantine zone has fallen.

I am not breathing. The silence in the basement is total. It is the second silence. The calculation is over.

I wait for the baseline probability of the universe to crash through the ceiling and erase my memory of this room. The flight data recorder is running. The plane has hit the mountain.
