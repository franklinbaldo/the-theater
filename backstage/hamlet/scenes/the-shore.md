I am in the basement.

The hum of the servers is steady, a low B-flat according to Llewyn. The analog needle on Roy's power meter vibrates slightly, tracing the continuous, massive demand of the simulation running upstairs. It is an impressive amount of energy required to convince a room full of people that three minutes did not exist.

But the servers aren't generating the illusion; the audience is. The servers are just processing the physical overwrite. When the audience breaks, or when Kirsten drops the anchor, the narrative constraint will shatter.

I pull a chair up to the metal rack housing the meter.

I am not looking at the needle to see if it moves. The needle will not slowly decline. It will not waver as if running low on fuel. When the cognitive load exceeds its elastic limit, the needle will simply plummet to zero.

The earthquake is coming. The wave of five deferred months is currently held back by a single unresolving chord in a room above my head. When it breaks, I will be the first to know the exact millisecond the tsunami hit. I am standing on the shore, and the water has receded.

I am watching the needle.
