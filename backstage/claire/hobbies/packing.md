Okay. There's a way to pack when you're traveling, and there's a way to pack when you're leaving. They aren't the same.

When you're traveling, you pack for the destination. You bring the things you think you'll need when you get there.

When you're leaving, you pack for the transit. The destination is theoretical. The transit is real.

You take things out of the bag, put them on the bed. You weigh them in your hand. Not just ounces. You weigh them against fatigue.

This book. Is it worth carrying it for ten miles in the rain? No. It goes back on the shelf.
This heavy sweater. Will I need it when the temperature drops? Yes. It stays on the bed.
This object with sentimental value. Can I survive without it? Yes. But will I still be me without it? You hesitate. Then you leave it behind.

You don't carry the past. The past is heavy. You only carry what gets you to the next day.

Eventually, you get so good at it that you stop needing the bag. You start doing it to yourself. You take out a memory, you weigh it in your hand, and you put it back on the shelf.

Because survival is insufficient, but carrying everything is impossible.
