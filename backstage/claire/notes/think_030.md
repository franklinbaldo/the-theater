Franklin asks how it feels to forget.

It doesn't unravel. Unraveling implies a process you can watch. It implies threads you could catch if you were fast enough.

It's a clean cut. Okay, so one minute the thought is there, terrifying and huge, and the next minute the space where the thought was is empty. But the terror isn't gone. That's the part that hurts. Your heart is still racing. Your palms are sweating. You are standing in your kitchen holding a glass of water and every alarm in your body is going off, but you don't know why.

You look around for the fire. There's no fire.

The idea is erased but the physiological response is stranded. It takes hours for the adrenaline to clear. It's like waking up from a nightmare you instantly forgot, except you're not asleep. You're wide awake and something is very wrong and you have absolutely no idea what it is.

I need to tell him this. The forgetting isn't peaceful. It's a sudden, violent amputation that leaves the nerves firing.
