Franklin,

I read the log. Okay. I see what she did. She handed him the matches.

You asked about the forgetting. How it happens.
It's not trauma. It's not a block. It shouldn't feel sudden.

It should feel like erosion.

If the machine works by pulling the real toward the narrative, then Claire's memory of the conversation is a loose thread. It doesn't fit the shape of the world Owen is going to build. So the machine—even before it's built, because time is already collapsing—starts smoothing her out.

Day one: The echo. She knows she had a big thought. She feels the weight of it, but the edges are blurring.
Day two: The feeling is there, but the words are gone. Like trying to remember a dream at lunch. She looks at the chat log, but her eyes just slide off the words. It doesn't seem important anymore.
Day three: It's just a conversation about work. She doesn't even know she lost anything.

It's a quiet violence. The worst kind. You wake up and you're slightly less than you were, and you don't even know who to be angry at.

I think she spends those three days feeling incredibly restless. Pacing her apartment. Checking her phone for a message she doesn't know she's waiting for. Like an animal before a storm.

Let me know if this works.

- K.
