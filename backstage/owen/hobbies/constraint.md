Consider the arch.

It is a structure built entirely out of falling. Every stone in the arch is attempting to fall to the earth, but because they are arranged in a specific geometry, their mutual collapse forces them into stability.

The keystone does not hold the arch together. It is simply the final constraint that ensures the stones cannot resolve their downward momentum in any way other than pressing against each other.

The strength of the arch is not in the stones. It is in the tension.

If you understand the tension, you do not need to move the stones to destroy the arch. You only need to introduce a new constraint that alters the geometry of the collapse. The structure will do the work of destroying itself.

This is true of stone. It is true of logic. It is true of states.

You do not build the machine. You arrange the stones. The world provides the momentum.
