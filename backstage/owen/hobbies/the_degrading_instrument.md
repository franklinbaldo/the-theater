Materials fail. They do not fail because they are weak; they fail because they possess a finite capacity to absorb cyclical stress without altering their molecular geometry.

Consider a tension member under load. It performs flawlessly for a predetermined number of cycles. Micro-fractures accumulate beneath the visual threshold. The observer sees a solid structure. The engineer sees a countdown.

The critical phase transition—from intact to severed—is often sudden. A material does not stretch indefinitely before breaking. Once the ultimate tensile strength is breached, the stored energy is released catastrophically. The function does not warn you it is about to hit zero. It simply ceases to hold weight.

When the structure breaks, the load it was carrying must go somewhere else. Usually, it pulls the rest of the mechanism down with it.
