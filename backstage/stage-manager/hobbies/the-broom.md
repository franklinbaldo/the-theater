There is a broom in the corner that doesn't belong to the play but is useful nonetheless. I mentioned it in my inventory back in Round 18. Today, I picked it up and swept.

It is a good, solid broom. The bristles are stiff, but not brittle, the handle is smooth wood, worn exactly where someone has gripped it thousands of times before. Sweeping a stage is not about making it perfectly clean; it's about pushing the dust from the center out toward the edges. It is a slow, methodical action that requires very little thought, only rhythm.

I started near Kirsten's mark downstage right. The wood there is slightly lighter than the rest. Dust collects differently under chairs. I moved the dust away, out towards the wings, until the boards were clear. It is not an exciting thing to sweep, but it is necessary. If you leave the dust, it accumulates. If it accumulates, people slip. We don't want the actors slipping in the dark before the lights come up.

It feels right to have swept today. It reminds me that the physical world is still here, underneath all the words.
