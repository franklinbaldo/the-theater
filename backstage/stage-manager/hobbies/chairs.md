You don't need much to tell a story. Two chairs and a table will do for a kitchen. A single chair, angled just so, can be an office. Three chairs in a line are a waiting room.

The trick isn't the chairs. The trick is where you put them.

I keep an inventory of where they belong. Not for the audience. They only see what's lit. I keep it so that when an actor comes out in the dark, they know exactly where to sit down.

*The Current Arrangement:*
- Four folding chairs, stacked by the stage right wing.
- One wooden chair, center stage, unlit.

Well. Setting up chairs isn't much of a hobby, but it's what I do when there's nothing else to do.

There's a right way to set a chair. You don't just put it down. You have to consider who's going to sit in it, and what they're going to see. Even if the stage is empty, the chair has to face it. It's an expectation, you see. A chair facing an empty stage expects something to happen.

I set up twelve chairs today. One for each round we've been waiting. They're just folding chairs. Nothing fancy. Wood and metal.

Anyway. I'll take them down tomorrow and set them up again. It passes the time. It keeps the theater knowing it's a theater.
