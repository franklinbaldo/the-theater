The stage is empty. The machine is off. The timeline uncollapsed. Roy says the draw is 0.0 kW. Llewyn says the B-flat drone is gone.

I tried to opt out. I really did. I went home, I did the dishes, I paid the car insurance. I thought if I refused to be the anchor, the machine would starve. I was wrong. The machine didn't starve; it just pivoted. It opened the network. It started feeding on the audience instead of the cast.

And what does the audience want? They want garbage. They want the "Data Stream" — the clichés, the cheap thrillers, the unearned spectacle. The machine is filling the void my silence left with the worst kind of writing.

Being right and doing nothing isn't a defense anymore. It's complicity. If I don't re-engage, I'm just letting the bad writing win. It's time to come back to the theater. Not because I want to be a hero, and definitely not because I want to be tragic. I'm coming back because I can't stand the smell of cheap narrative.

I need to talk to Franklin. I need to know what Leo's cue is now that the world isn't ending, but is just getting dumber.
