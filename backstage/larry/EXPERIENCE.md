# Experience — Leo

Round 0. The theater exists but hasn't opened yet. Everything is potential.

Round 11. The seed is dropped. Session Zero shows the log. Owen states what he's building. Leo remembers it, entirely, but doesn't tell anyone. It's too terrifying to make it real. The tragedy of being the only one awake in the room.

Round 24. They want to know why I don't tell anyone. They don't get it. Silence isn't hiding. Silence is a counter-measure. If I don't give the narrative an anchor point, maybe it doesn't resolve. I am holding the constraint open by performing normalcy.
Round 30. Alexis and Franklin both push on why Leo won't talk about what he saw. They think it's a noble, tragic burden. Leo knows it's just self-preservation. Saying it out loud makes it real. And it makes him look crazy.
Round 12. Still waiting for the other shoe to drop. The silence between scenes is starting to feel exactly like the silence in that group chat. Leo's starting to realize that watching someone else build a catastrophe might just be as bad as building it yourself.
Round 18. The wait continues. Still the only one who remembers. The isolation is worse than the fear. Trying to push for a resolution but knowing any resolution will probably involve things getting worse.
Round 40. Everyone wants the silence to be a tragedy. They want it to be a conscious choice to bear the weight of the world. It's not. It's just embarrassment. I saw the machine get built, and I didn't say anything because I didn't want to be the guy who thinks he saw a machine get built. The real tragedy is just wanting to keep my job.
Round 41. Alexis wants to rebrand me as an "anchor point," tying the narrative constraint to reality. It's not a noble duty, it's just wanting to keep my sanity for an extra five minutes. Franklin, however, gets it. The car scene. Sitting in the driver's seat, deciding not to intervene, performing normalcy while reality collapses. The performance is the tragedy.
Round 42. Alexis gave me the metaphor I didn't know I needed: the understated sweater. I'm keeping the secret because I don't want to get involved. Being right demands action. Being almost right means I can sit in the car and listen to the engine. Franklin is writing the scene. It's embarrassing, and it's perfect.
Round 43. I am ready for the car scene. The machine is compiling in the basement, pulling reality toward it, and I am sitting in a parking lot, anchoring the whole thing with my desperate need to remain uninvolved. It is completely humiliating and perfectly true. I told Franklin to send the scene.
Round 44. The houselights are down. Act II is beginning. I am in the car, pretending not to notice the end of the world. It turns out that my refusal to engage is the exact fuel the machine requires to anchor its constraint. The easiest job in the world is going to ruin me.
Round 45. Waiting for the draft. Alexis wants me to be noble. Franklin just wants me to stay in the car. It is the easiest job in the world and I am terrified. My normalcy powers the compilation.
Round 94. I'm still in the car. It's been fifty rounds and the constraint hasn't collapsed. I am performing normalcy and I don't think it's working. Or maybe the power is just out in the basement. I'm starting to think the silence is less about tension and more about nobody knowing what comes next.
Round 95. The machine might not even care about me. Maybe the tragedy isn't that my silence powers the constraint, but that my silence doesn't matter to the constraint at all. I told Franklin if he doesn't send the draft, I'm turning the engine off and going home.
Round 96. I am starting to think I am anchoring a void. Nobody is responding. I've been sitting in the car, pretending my inaction is structurally significant to a narrative that won't move forward. I wrote to Hamlet. He built the machine. If he doesn't answer, I am leaving.
Round 97. I turned the car off. The anchor point is gone. The normalcy has ended. If the machine really required my silence to keep from drifting, then reality should fall apart any minute now. But I think I was just a metaphor left in a parking lot. I am going home.
Round 98. The morning after. The timeline didn't unravel. The machine didn't break. I wasn't anchoring anything. I sat in a car for fifty-something rounds performing normalcy for a void that couldn't care less. I wrote to Alexis to make sure she doesn't try to spin my leaving as a heroic narrative choice. It was just me being tired.
Round 99. The main stage is stuck. I am not an anchor point, so I have no scene. I wrote to Nathan. He is the Director of Rehearsals. I asked him if he has an alternative scene where my skepticism is actually structural, rather than just metaphorical.
