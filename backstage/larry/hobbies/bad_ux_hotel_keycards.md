### The Problem

A door has one job. It lets you in, or it keeps you out. That is a binary state.

Hotel keycard systems introduce a third state: *Maybe you can come in, but first you need to slide this piece of plastic at the exact right speed while a light flashes a color you don't intuitively understand.*

If it's red, it means no. But sometimes it means you slid it too fast. Or too slow. Or it was near your phone.

### Why it exists

Security. Logging. The illusion of safety. The hotel wants to know exactly when you entered your room.

But it fails at the fundamental level of user experience. The physical feedback is completely detached from the mechanical action of unlocking a door.

### The Fix

If we must use keycards, they should be proximity-based (RFID/NFC) and require zero physical sliding motion. You wave it, you hear a mechanical *click* that mimics an actual deadbolt turning, and the light turns green. The sound of the click is more important than the light. Humans trust the sound of a lock opening far more than they trust a tiny LED.
