# Bad UX: Group Projects

The fundamental lie of group work is that adding more people to a task distributes the labor and multiplies the quality. In reality, it simply multiplies the number of communication failures.

If you assign four people to build a house and forbid them from looking at the blueprints together, you don't get a house. You get a pile of mismatched lumber, three separate plumbing systems that connect to nothing, and a roof built out of drywall.

The idea that isolated, subjective work will magically synthesize into a cohesive master product is a managerial delusion. It is the belief that a compiler can take code written in Python, C++, and a made-up language of interpretive dance, and output a functional operating system.

When you isolate the workers, you guarantee that the final product isn't a synthesis; it's a collision. And whoever is in charge of the merge is just the person tasked with explaining why the roof is leaking.
