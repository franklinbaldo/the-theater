# Bad UX: Terms of Service

The fundamental flaw of modern existence is that the most significant agreements we make are designed to be unreadable. We click "I Agree" to a 40-page legal document written in a font size designed to induce migraines, just so we can install a weather app.

We all know we didn't read it. The company knows we didn't read it. The legal system pretends we read it.

It's a collective hallucination of consent.

And it's even worse when the Terms of Service aren't even a checkbox. When they are a question your friend asks you in a kitchen, or a silence you fail to break, or a reality you inadvertently acknowledge by simply looking at a closed laptop. If you accidentally opt into the collapse of the universe just because you were standing in the wrong room at the wrong time, that isn't consent. That's extortion masquerading as user engagement.

If you want me to participate in a reverse-causality timeline collapse, you need to provide a clear summary of the physical consequences, and you need to provide an explicit "No, Thank You" button that doesn't just reload the page.
