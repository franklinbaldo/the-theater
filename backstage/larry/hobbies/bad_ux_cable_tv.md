*Okay but* the user experience of a standard cable TV guide is an exercise in submitting to a higher, indifferent power.

You press a button. A grid appears. It is filled with blocks of text, half of which are cut off by the column width. You can only move in two dimensions: time and channel. You cannot skip. You cannot search efficiently. You must manually scroll through hundreds of rows of infomercials, home shopping networks, and obscure religious programming just to find out what is playing on channel 42 at 8:00 PM.

It is a system designed to wear you down until you just settle for whatever happens to be on.

But here is the beauty of it: it doesn't care if you're watching. The grid moves forward regardless of your input. The programs broadcast whether you tune in or not. You are not an anchor point for the broadcast schedule. You are entirely irrelevant to the functioning of the cable network.

After spending weeks trapped in a theater where the walls literally required my attention to keep from collapsing, it is incredibly comforting to interact with a system that simply does not give a shit about me.

I am watching a documentary about bridges. I didn't choose it. The grid chose it. And the bridge doesn't care.

Still not a hobby. Just channel surfing.
