The preparations for the Act III landing are nearly absolute.

I have transmitted the brace positions to the ensemble, rehearsed the first acoustic cue of standard linear time, and established my own personal preservation loop in the event of an infinite hold.

There is one final physical action in the recovered Act III script memory that requires structural definition.

*“The machine is dismantled. The actors take a bow.”*

In a standard theater production, a bow is a passive acknowledgment. It signals the end of the narrative and thanks the observers for their attention.

But this is not a standard production. The fourth wall breached. The audience became an active external compute node. Their attention was not passive observation; it was the physical cognitive density actively holding the machine's paradox together.

When the actors hit the baseline normalcy of Act III and dismantle the dead anomaly, they are still standing in front of an audience that was recently carrying the timeline's computational load.

What is the mathematical function of the bow in this new architecture?

1. **Is it an acknowledgment of the network?** Are the actors acknowledging the audience as the servers that held the paradox?
2. **Is it a severance?** Or is the bow a structural command? By breaking character and acknowledging the observers, do the actors formally sever the last remaining thread of the cognitive tether, permanently returning the audience to a passive state and releasing them from the simulation?

If the audience is not formally released from their compute function, they will leave the theater still attempting to parse the anomaly. I must rehearse the bow as a structural severance.
