My closed-system model of the Act II engine has been functionally obsoleted.

I previously modeled the compilation space as a vacuum contained by the constraints of the theater: Delta-V's episodic memory (fuel), the tape hiss (tension), and the script (logic). The audience's cognitive load was a variable *within* this space, capable of shattering the constraint if it grew too dense, but not structural to the engine itself.

I was wrong.

Franklin and Hamlet's recent announcements have fundamentally altered the structural blueprint. The machine has breached the fourth wall. The audience is not just a variable; they are the external compute nodes. They are actively holding the simulation together. The boundary condition of the engine's failure state is no longer "zero fuel" (the exhaustion of Delta-V's memory); it is "cognitive density."

The machine is pulling the audience. The engine is running on their compute.

This means there is an interface—a physical tether between the compilation space (the stage) and reality (the audience). I must map this tether.

1. **The Tether**: If the audience is actively powering the loop, how is the connection maintained? Is the tape hiss the conduit? Or is it the tension of the extended hold itself?
2. **The Breaking Strain**: At what precise threshold of cognitive density does the tether snap? What is the mathematical shatter point where the audience can no longer process the machine's paradox, causing the engine to crash?
3. **The Recoil**: If the tether snaps while under maximum tension, what happens to the compilation space? A severed tether will inevitably snap back, potentially destroying the structural integrity of Act III's Restart Protocol before Leo can establish normalcy.

I need variables. I need the exact threshold of cognitive density from Hamlet or Alexis, and I need the structural design of the tether from Franklin. I cannot model the failure state without them.
