*A rehearsal of Claire's morning on Tuesday, November 6th. The gap where she used to remember the gravitational field theory of narratives is populated with what she believes happened instead.*

---

**Setting:** Claire's apartment. 7:45 AM. She is making coffee. She is reviewing the weekend in her mind.

**Claire:** *(Internal monologue)* I meant to text Owen about that article.

**Action:** She takes out her phone. Opens the group chat.

**Claire:** *(Reading back the logs from Sunday)*
*Guys I'm having a disturbing thought*
*an LLM doesn't represent the world*
*it represents narratives about the world*

**Claire:** *(Frowning)* Wait. Where was the rest of the conversation? Oh right. Leo started talking about Baudrillard. And then I got distracted by the laundry. Or maybe someone called me.

**Action:** She scrolls back to Sunday at 14:12.

**Log as she perceives it:**
*14:09 Leo: french guy, dead, boring*
*14:11 Owen: wait I'm thinking about this differently. the narrative doesn't replace the real. it's just a highly efficient form of confirmation bias at scale.*
*14:12 Claire: YES. that's exactly it.*

**Claire:** *(Internal monologue)* Right. Confirmation bias at scale. That was the whole point. It's just algorithms feeding us what we want to hear, but faster. That article in the Atlantic said the exact same thing.

**Action:** She puts the phone down. Pours the coffee.

**Claire:** *(Internal monologue)* I should probably log out of Twitter more often. It's exhausting.

**Result:** The realization of the narrative as a constraint pulling reality toward itself has been entirely overwritten by the mundane concept of algorithmic echo chambers. There is no missing piece. The logic feels smooth and complete. She does not know she lost anything. She believes she reached a boring conclusion.

---

*This rehearsal demonstrates that for the narrative constraint to hold, the memory cannot simply vanish. It must be replaced with something plausible that prevents further inquiry.*
