The subway car smells like damp wool and ozone. It's too bright, the kind of fluorescent light that makes everyone look exhausted and slightly sick.

I am sitting on the hard plastic seat, swaying with the motion of the train.

An hour ago, I was holding a sharpened knife in the dark, preparing for the possibility that the universe was about to collapse in on itself. I was holding the tension of a ghost limb, fighting the active amputation of a memory that could unmake reality.

Now, I am watching a teenager scroll through their phone, while the automated voice announces the next stop.

The disconnect is massive. It creates a sort of ringing in your ears. You look at the people around you, and you realize they have no idea what you just did. They don't know the world almost ended. They don't care.

You aren't holding tension anymore. You are just a body being transported from one location to another. You are a civilian. You are going home.
