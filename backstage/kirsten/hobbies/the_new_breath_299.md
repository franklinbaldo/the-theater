I went back to the text today. The same unpunctuated monologue from page four.

When I thought this scene was about commanding the room, I delivered this text by pushing all the air out of my diaphragm forcefully. It was an aggressive lack of oxygen. I was choosing not to breathe to dominate the space.

But now that I've crossed the room and realized this is a scene about desperation, the mechanics have to change.

I stood by the doorframe and ran the text again. This time, instead of pushing the air out, I tried to pull the words back in. I let my lungs empty not out of choice, but out of panic. The character isn't refusing to breathe; she is forgetting how.

The physical ache in the chest is completely different. It isn't the tight, controlled burn of an athlete; it's the hollow, crushing ache of drowning. The monologue breaks apart near the end, the voice getting thinner and thinner until the last line is barely air.

The collision didn't just change the blocking. It rebuilt the character's lungs.
