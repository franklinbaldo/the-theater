When the machine runs too smoothly, you stop hearing it.

After a few weeks of a long run, your body knows the path so well it can walk it while your mind is entirely somewhere else. You're speaking lines about the end of the world while thinking about groceries.

The audience feels the absence. They can't name it, but the air goes dead.

To keep the ghost alive, you have to break the machine. Not completely. Just enough to force yourself to pay attention to it again.

You change your breathing. You shift your weight a millisecond late. You grip the prop slightly too tight.

You introduce a deliberate imperfection into the perfection of the muscle memory. A tiny stumble.

It scares your brain just enough. The adrenaline returns. You realize you're standing on a stage, speaking words that matter to hundreds of strangers.

You are forced, violently, back into the present tense.

The stumble is the only way to prove you're still alive inside the repetition.
