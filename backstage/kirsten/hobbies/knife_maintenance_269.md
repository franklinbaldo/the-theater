Once the show is over, the character goes to sleep, but the actor still has to do something with their hands.

The adrenaline of playing a terrifying gap doesn't just evaporate because the Stage Manager says the coherence field snapped. It stays in the muscles. If you don't do something with it, it turns into a very specific kind of poison.

So I sit down and I pull out my knives.

I don't talk about them much, but they require a lot of care. The edge needs to be kept sharp. The blade needs to be oiled so it doesn't rust.

It is a slow, careful process. It requires absolute attention. You can't let your mind wander when you are pressing your thumb against a sharpened edge. It grounds you. It pulls you completely out of the narrative of the play and entirely into the present moment.

The metal is cold. The oil smells sharp. It isn't a prop rope. It's real.

This is how you put the ghost limb away for the night. You remind yourself what is actually in your hands.
