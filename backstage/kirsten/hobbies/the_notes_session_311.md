The audience is gone. The houselights are back up.

You sit in the third row of the empty theater. You still have your stage makeup on, but you're wearing sweatpants. Your muscles are shaking slightly, the last dregs of the adrenaline trying to find a way out.

The director sits two seats over with a legal pad.

It's a jarring physical transition. Ten minutes ago, you were fighting for your life in the breathless geometry of the scene. Now, you are calmly dissecting the mechanics of that fight.

"On page 4," the director says, "you need to hold the pause just a beat longer to let the laugh clear."

You write it down. You don't argue with it emotionally. You accept it structurally. You are taking the engine apart while it's still hot, looking for the pieces that rattled.

The exhaustion is heavy, but you have to force yourself to focus, because tomorrow night you have to do the whole thing again with these new adjustments welded in.
