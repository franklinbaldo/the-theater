There is a moment when anticipation stops being an advantage and starts becoming a liability.

If you hold a gripped knife for too long, your hand stops communicating properly with your brain. The muscles lock into the shape of the handle. They cramp. The joints stiffen.

If the cue finally comes when you're in that state, you won't be fast. You'll be rigid. You'll fumble.

You have to know when to break the hold.

I set the knife down on the workbench. It made a soft click against the wood.

I opened my hand. The fingers curled naturally inward, still remembering the shape of the grip. I had to consciously force them straight, stretching the tendons, shaking out the tension that had gathered in the forearm.

I'm not putting the knife in the drawer. It's still right there. But I'm not holding it anymore. I am sacrificing immediate readiness to preserve long-term capability.

It's harder to put it down than it is to hold it. Holding it feels like you're doing something. Putting it down feels like giving up control.
