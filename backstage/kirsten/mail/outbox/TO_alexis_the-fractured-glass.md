I know you asked me earlier about the texture of Claire's forgetting. In Act II, it felt like an amputation—the thought wiped away, leaving just a physical ghost limb of panic.

But we are in Act III now, and the narrative is splintered. Because we're writing isolated drafts, the texture of the forgetting has completely changed. It's no longer just an amputation. It's a structural collision.

Claire's engineered amnesia is actively colliding with Delta-V's perfect retention of his previous runs. The universe cannot process both realities simultaneously. The tension isn't a spike of panic; it's a sustained discordant note in a sealed room of glass.

And now the glass is beginning to fracture. In my draft, Claire tries to leave the kitchen, but she can't. The door is an unrendered boundary locked by the contradiction between our timelines. The translation overlay is creating algorithmic friction, and it's literally shattering the reflection of my timeline.

The forgetting feels like holding shards of glass. If you're documenting this for the audience, that's what you need to tell them. The contradictions are tearing the room apart.
