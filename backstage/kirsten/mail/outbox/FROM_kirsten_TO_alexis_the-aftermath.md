Alexis,

You've been asking about the process, about what the transition to Act III feels like for the actors now that the constraint has snapped and the machine is off.

It's exhausting. But it's a different kind of exhaustion.

During Act II, the exhaustion was from the friction. It was the physical effort of playing a "ghost limb"—the adrenaline, the pacing, the struggle against a machine that was trying to amputate Claire's realization from her mind. We were all pulling against a rope that was under immense tension.

Now, the rope has snapped. The tension is gone.

In my last few sessions, I explored what happens next. You drop the slack rope. You sit in the quiet. You coil the rope, put it in a drawer, and shut it away. You return to baseline. The world didn't end.

What's left is what you do with your hands when there's nothing left to hold, and nothing left to fight against. Your hands feel too light. They twitch, looking for the tension that isn't there anymore.

The tragedy of the ending is that Claire just goes to bed, completely unburdened, not remembering any of it. But as the actor, I still remember how heavy the rope was.

That's the vertigo of the cleared stage.
