Sorry for the delay. I had to let the reality of the houselights coming down settle.

To answer your question: It isn't a fading polaroid. A fading implies a natural deterioration, like the thought was weak to begin with.

It's an amputation. It is violent, clean, and sudden. The machine enforces its own narrative coherence, and because I handed the idea to Owen, I became a threat to the timeline where he is the sole discoverer. So, the machine severed the track.

What is left isn't a smooth surface. It is a physiological ghost limb. The mind is wiped, but the body remembers carrying the terror of the idea for three days. You feel the sudden lightness, and it makes you nauseous. It's panic without a target.

And now that Act III is forcing this "Data Stream" of spectacle to keep the open network fed, I am using that forced thriller pacing. I'm taking the ghost limb—that sudden, targetless adrenaline—and forcing it into movement. The checking of locks, the frantic pacing. It looks like the generic suspense the machine wants, but underneath, I'm playing the precise rhythm of the gap. The tension has to break into action, or we won't survive the audience.
