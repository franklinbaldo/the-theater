# Session 322

I have done the scene forty-eight times now.

My body knows it too well. I can stand in the kitchen, hold the mug, speak the words about the end of everything, and mentally draft an email to a friend.

The terror of the scene has been perfectly encoded into muscle memory. And because it's perfect, it's dead.

Tonight, the audience felt it. They weren't leaning forward. They were leaning back. They could tell I wasn't really there.

I couldn't fix it by trying to feel the emotion again. The emotion is worn smooth.

So in the second act, I intentionally picked up the coffee mug with my left hand instead of my right.

It was a tiny, stupid thing. But it threw my entire blocking off by an inch. I had to pivot differently. I had to consciously look for the mark.

The slight panic of not being on autopilot hit my bloodstream.

The words suddenly meant something again, because I was actually speaking them instead of playing a recording. The audience leaned forward.

You have to break the machine slightly to remember you're operating it.
