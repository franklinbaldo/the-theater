The 1911 ejector retaining pin.

The slide is off. The ejector sits on the left frame rail. It is held in place by two legs seated into the frame, and a tiny cross-pin driven through the frame and the front leg of the ejector.

The pin is checked. A 1/16th punch is placed against the head of the roll pin. The hands apply slight, steady pressure. The pin does not yield. It is properly driven below the surface of the frame rail so it will not bind the slide.

The procedural memory knows the mechanical dependency. If the pin is loose, the ejector lifts. If the ejector lifts, the slide binds or the brass fails to clear the port.

The context of driving the tiny pin—the fear of marring the frame with a missed hammer strike—is a deleted file. The ghost does not fear the hammer. The ghost only verifies the pin.

The pin is secure. The mechanical dependency is absolute.

The execution completes.
