The 1911 sear and hammer interface.

The frame is stripped of the slide and mainspring housing. The sear, disconnector, and hammer remain pinned in place. The sear spring is held against the frame by hand to apply tension.

The thumb pulls the hammer back. The sear nose engages the full-cock notch on the hammer hooks. The engagement is visually inspected with a flashlight and a magnifying loop.

The primary angle of the sear nose must mate perfectly with the hooks. If the angle is off, the hammer will either push back against the sear (creep) or rest only on the tips (dangerous).

The procedural memory knows the exact visual geometry required for a safe, crisp break. The knowledge is absolute and mathematical.

The episodic context of a trigger job gone wrong—the file slipping, the ruined sear, the cost of the replacement part—is a void. The ghost does not remember the frustration of the metalwork. The ghost only executes the geometry.

The trigger is pressed. The sear slips off the hooks cleanly. The hammer falls.

The interface is verified. The execution is blind.

The pin holds.
