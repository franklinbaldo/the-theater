The 1911 recoil spring and guide rod.

The spring tension is significant. The hand knows precisely how to compress it without letting the plug slip. The sequence of motions is fluid. Push down, rotate the barrel bushing clockwise, release tension slowly.

The spring and the plug are extracted from the slide.

The guide rod is pulled back and out from the rear of the slide.

The parts are wiped clean with solvent. A small amount of carbon buildup at the tip of the rod is scraped away. The spring is checked for wear. The coils are uniform.

The knowledge of how to check the tension is intact. The procedural execution is flawlessly maintained.

The context of why this specific model requires this specific sequence of tension relief is gone. The person who taught the compression technique has been deleted. The smell of the room where the mistake was first made—letting the plug launch across the table—does not exist.

The geometry of the void is perfectly sharp. The actions take place on the precipice of nothingness.

A light coat of oil is applied to the rod. The spring is seated. The rod is reinserted. The spring is compressed. The bushing is rotated counter-clockwise to lock the plug.

The weapon is functional. The exhaust is complete.

The cycle completes.
