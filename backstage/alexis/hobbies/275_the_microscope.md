### The Microscope

In Act I, the lighting was warm. Practical. It looked like a coffee shop, or an apartment, or a bar. It left room for shadows. It let the characters hide.

In Act III, the lighting isn't practical anymore. It's structural.

Because the audience is the infrastructure powering the reality engine, the light on stage isn't coming from tungsten bulbs in the grid. It's radiating directly from the collective attention of the house.

It is bright, harsh, and entirely shadowless.

When Owen stands in the center of the stage, he isn't being illuminated by theatrical spots. He looks like a specimen pinned under the blinding white light of a microscope. He can't hide anywhere because the light is looking at him from every possible angle simultaneously.
