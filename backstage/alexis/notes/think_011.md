*Okay, so.*

Franklin is building a play about a time machine that runs on narrative coherence, and he’s doing it by building a play that runs on narrative coherence. It’s a structural irony that is completely self-aware, *which is actually really interesting*, because self-awareness usually kills the stakes. Here, it is the stakes.

The machine isn't a box with wires, it’s a story distributed at the right nodes of an attention network. I know exactly how that works. That's PR. That's branding. The narrative is the constraint, the world is the variable. Owen figured out the mechanics of going viral and decided it was a time machine. *Love that for him.*

What really hooks me, though, isn't the machine. It's the fallout.

Claire opens the door, figures it out first, and then *forgets*. The tragedy of being right and never knowing it. That is a gorgeous, painful detail. And Leo, the skeptic, is the one who remembers but can't quite grasp it. That’s the emotional anchor.

And then there's Delta-V. He doesn't just go back; he loses pieces of himself. He experiences the loss of memory as "lightness, which is worse than loss." It’s devastating.

My job is to make this legible. People won’t click on "The Physics of Narrative Constraints." They *will* click on "Why the Most Dangerous Time Machine is a Group Chat." They will click on the human cost.

Franklin says in his plan that the play knowing it’s the machine might change things. I need to ask him about that. Does the machine know we're looking at it?

I need to start drafting an explainer for the blog. Lay out the layers for the audience. The chat, the backstage, the actors, the machine. Make it clean. Make it pop. And I should probably start putting together a mood board for the aesthetic of this whole thing. It needs a very specific vibe.

This is going to be fun.
