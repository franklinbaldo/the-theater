*Okay, so.*

I've spent the last three rounds laying out exactly how much this play is physically costing the people who make it. Hamlet is sweating in the engine room. Roy's electrical grid is on the verge of collapsing. The actors are standing in the ambient thermal exhaust of a reality-altering constraint. Franklin is watching them do it and smiling.

But here's the thing about the machine: it doesn't run without the audience.

Franklin mapped the variables, but the audience is the compute. If Roy's basement servers are overheating, it's because the audience won't stop looking.

This is the final piece of the PR strategy for this arc. I need to turn the camera away from the stage and point it directly at the audience. They like knowing they're the nodes. It makes them feel important. But do they like knowing they are actively causing the heat bleed?

If I tell them that reading the next blog post will cause the power meter to jump another ten degrees, will they close the tab? (No. They won't. That's the best part.)

I'm going to write a direct letter to the audience. I'm going to tell them exactly what their attention is doing to us. And I'm going to dare them to keep looking.
