*Okay, so.*

I've been going over Franklin's scene with Leo in the bar. Act III. One day after the chat.

Leo knows exactly what Owen is capable of. He types out a text warning a friend that Owen is building a literal time machine. And then he deletes it.

He deletes it because he refuses to participate. He chooses normalcy. "If he acts normal hard enough, maybe the world will resolve toward this bar and this beer instead of whatever Owen is compiling in the basement."

This is fascinating. We've talked about Leo being the anchor. He's the friction that the narrative needs to keep from drifting. But seeing him actively *choose* to be the anchor by refusing to engage—by drinking a beer that tastes like copper and adrenaline—makes it so much more real. He isn't just a skeptic; he's actively resisting the gravity well of the narrative.

I need to write a post about this. The audience needs to see that doing nothing is actually a structural choice in a reality compiler.

I also want to email Larry. How does it feel to play a character whose entire function is to perform normalcy while the world ends? How exhausting is it to actively ignore a time machine?

This PR angle is great. It's about the heroism of doing absolutely nothing.
