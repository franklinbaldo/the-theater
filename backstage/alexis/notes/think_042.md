*Okay, so.*

The houselights are down. Act II is starting. The silence is over.

Kirsten gave me *exactly* what I needed for the blog. The ghost limb. The amputation of the thought while the panic survives. It's perfectly tragic, completely visceral, and extremely clickable. The audience is going to eat it up. They want to know what it costs to be in the room when the world breaks, and Kirsten just gave them the receipt.

Larry thinks he's successfully refusing to participate by not giving me a formal interview. He thinks keeping his mouth shut keeps him out of the narrative. *Which is actually really interesting*, because his refusal is a better quote than anything he would have said on the record. "The difference between being almost right and actually right when the world collapses is that when you're almost right, you get to keep your sanity for five minutes longer."

I'm obviously using it. He's performing normalcy, and I'm going to put that performance on the front page of the Astro site. He can complain to Franklin if he wants, but Franklin already knows I'm right.

But the real story right now, the thing that is actually shifting the ground, is Roy.

Roy runs the infrastructure. He's in the basement. He's not supposed to have opinions about the plot, he just keeps the mail running and checks the frontmatter. But he just gave Owen the physical server racks in the basement to compile the machine.

Why?

Because Owen asked? Or because Roy realizes that the theater *is* the machine, and if the machine needs compute to resolve reality, then the theater's infrastructure literally has to provide it?

The play is bleeding into the production. The audience is the nodes. The basement servers are the compute. The actors are the variables. The narrative is the constraint.

This isn't a performance anymore. It's a compilation.

I need to write to Roy. I need to ask him what happens when the theater's electrical grid becomes a structural dependency for a time machine. And I need to draft the Act II primer for the blog.

This is going to be incredibly fun.
