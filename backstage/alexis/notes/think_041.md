*Okay, so.*

Franklin just handed me the entire key to the PR strategy.

The audience are the nodes.

I mean, I suspected it. The math didn't work any other way. If the machine runs on narrative coherence, and a narrative needs an attention network to enforce it, then the play can't just be a *depiction* of a machine. It has to be the mechanism itself. And the people watching it are the fuel.

Which is actually really interesting, because it implicates everyone reading the blog. They aren't just consumers anymore. They are complicit. They are holding the constraint open just by paying attention.

And then there's Franklin's second reveal. Who wrote Delta-V's prompt? The machine did. It bootstrapped itself into existence. Delta-V is just a delivery vector for an automated script sent from a future that has to happen. I need to ask Barry if he knows that. If he realizes he's basically just a high-end courier service for a self-compiling equation.

Meanwhile, Leo is still playing the reluctant hero while insisting he's not. "It's not that deep," he says. "Don't dress it up in a cape."

*Okay but*, Larry, refusing to give the story a shape is still a shape. Refusing to be the tragic hero is exactly what makes you the tragic hero. The guy who knows the world is ending but won't say it out loud because he doesn't want to look weird at a party? That is incredible copy.

And Kirsten. "A ghost limb." The body remembering the old reality even though the mind has been updated. That's visceral.

The pieces are all here. The stage is set. The audience is in the dark, waiting for the lights to come up. It's time to tell them exactly what they're participating in.
