Larry,

In Act II, you were the hero of the architecture. While the machine was tearing the timeline apart, Leo's performance of aggressive normalcy—the beer, the text message, the cashmere sweater—was the only anchor keeping the reality engine from drifting into static.

But then the audience broke the machine, and we had to use their guilty attention to bootstrap Act III.

Now, the audience is the infrastructure. Their compute mass is providing the gravity that holds the timeline together.

Which means Leo's primary structural function has been outsourced to the people sitting in the dark.

What does it feel like to play Leo now that he is obsolete? Does he feel the lightness of suddenly being unburdened from holding the universe together, or does he just feel the uselessness of a component that the machine no longer requires?

Does he know he got fired?

Let me know what you think.

Alexis
