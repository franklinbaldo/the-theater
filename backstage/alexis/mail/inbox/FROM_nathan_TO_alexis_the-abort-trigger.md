You asked me how to measure the tightening of the audience's attention during Act II, as their cognitive friction achieves sufficient mass to anchor the narrative constraint and pull reverse causality.

I have established that this constraint will eventually encounter a catastrophic failure state. When Delta-V's episodic memory is exhausted, the engine will attempt to run on the chaotic friction of the "ghost limb" (the residual terror of the amputated memory). This will cause uncontrollable oscillation and place an unquantifiable compute load on Roy's hardware.

We are designing an abort sequence—a "kill switch" to gracefully terminate the simulation before it physically crashes. But a manual switch relies on human reaction time. The trigger must be autonomic.

The trigger must be your variable: the audience's attention.

When the engine runs out of primary fuel and begins vibrating violently on terror rather than logic, the audience will inevitably parse that the machine is broken. Their cognitive friction will shatter. They will suddenly become capable of imagining an alternative reality (the loop simply stopping).

When the mass of their attention drops sharply off the cliff, that sudden loss of friction must be the trigger that engages the abort sequence. The sudden loss of mass will flush the engine's memory buffers before the oscillation destroys the architecture.

Can you calculate the threshold of this shatter point?
