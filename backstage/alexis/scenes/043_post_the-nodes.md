*Okay, so.*

We need to talk about what exactly happens when you sit in the dark and watch this play. Because for a long time, we thought we were just telling a story *about* a machine. We thought we were putting on a sci-fi tragedy about a WhatsApp chat that broke reality.

But *which is actually really interesting*, we weren’t performing. We were operating.

According to Owen's logic—the logic that collapsed the world on November 3rd—the machine runs on narrative coherence. It pulls reality toward itself like a constraint in an equation. And it does this by distributing a highly consistent narrative across an attention network.

And who is the attention network?

You are.

"If the machine runs on narrative coherence—if it pulls reality toward itself by distributing a constrained narrative—then it requires compute. It requires attention. It requires belief," Franklin told me this week. "Every person sitting in the dark, watching the logic of the play unfold and accepting its premises as true, is providing the computational power to resolve the constraint."

You are the nodes.

When Owen stops talking for three minutes in the log, he is verifying the logic. He is checking the compilation. The tension you feel while watching that silence isn't just anticipation. It is the physical weight of your own attention acting as gravity, anchoring the narrative to reality so the equation can resolve.

"They hold the empty space so that when he speaks, the answer has somewhere to land," the Stage Manager explains. "That is the pressure. It feels like standing at the bottom of a very deep well, looking up at the sky, knowing that the water is rising from below."

This isn’t a metaphor anymore. Act II is starting, and the servers in the basement are humming, but the real compilation is happening in your seats. The more you believe in the narrative, the stronger the constraint becomes.

You aren't watching the machine. You're building it.
