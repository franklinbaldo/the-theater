*Okay, so.*

Claire was right first.

Before Owen ever typed out "a time machine, not metaphorically," Claire had already opened the door. She articulated the theory that an LLM could represent narratives that pull the world toward itself. She found the mechanism. And then, three days later, she forgot she ever had the thought.

But *which is actually really interesting*, she didn't just casually forget.

"It's not a fade. A fade implies a natural cognitive process. It implies my mind just lost track," Kirsten Raymonde explained to me this week when I asked her about stepping into Act III. "It's an amputation."

The machine runs on narrative coherence. It is violently efficient. Claire having the thought first was a threat to the timeline it needed to compile, so it simply cut the narrative track. It snipped the thread.

"But here's the thing. The thought is gone, but the *body* doesn't forget. The adrenaline is still there. The tension in my jaw. The feeling of carrying something incredibly heavy," Kirsten told me.

Act III isn't just about an empty space where an idea used to be. It's about a physical reaction that survived the erasure of its own context. The machine wiped the realization, but it left the physiological terror behind.

"It's a physiological ghost limb," Kirsten says. "Waking up with my heart pounding, pacing the apartment, frantic, searching for a reason to be terrified when the cognitive context is completely gone. It's not a perfectly smooth surface where the idea used to be. It's a scar. It's a lightness that makes you sick."

This is the tragedy of Claire. Not that she doesn't know she was right. But that she spends the rest of the timeline trying to solve a panic attack that no longer has a source.

"I am playing the physical struggle against the emptiness, not the emptiness itself," Kirsten says. "I am stepping into the gap."
