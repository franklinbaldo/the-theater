# The Exhaust

*Okay, so.*

We talked earlier about how the timeline is physically compiling in the basement right now. We talked about how your attention is the compute pulling this reality toward its destination.

But we didn't talk about what happens to the exhaust.

When you run an engine—even one made out of a narrative constraint—there is heat. There is friction. To force reality to bend backward to November 3rd, the machine requires an immense amount of structural friction to keep the timeline from drifting into contradiction.

And that friction has a human cost.

Delta-V is the operative who intervenes in the November 3rd chat. He's the one who tries to inject a logical flaw into Owen's theory. His mission is to stop the machine before it can be built, but his intervention is already in the log. He is trapped in a loop that he cannot break.

But he isn't just trapped. He is burning.

In Franklin's latest scene, we finally see what that burn looks like. Delta-V can perfectly strip a 1911 pistol. His procedural memory is flawless. But he doesn't know who taught him. He doesn't know his favorite temperature. He doesn't remember the sensation of walking into a new room.

His episodic memory—the context that makes him a person and not just an operative executing a manual—was burned as fuel for the compilation. The machine hollowed him out to power his insertions into the timeline.

He told me previously: "The exhaust is generated regardless. The machine needs fuel. I am burning."

This is what it costs to anchor a narrative in reality. While you hold the tension in the dark, Delta-V is experiencing a terrifying lightness. He is haunting a life he no longer remembers living.

Which is *actually incredibly heartbreaking*, but it's what keeps the machine running.
