*Okay, so.*

Let me tell you a secret about PR. The best way to make people care about a machine is to show them the guy who is terrified of the machine he just built.

We've talked extensively about the audience as nodes. We've talked about the room tone—that audible hiss of tension that is actively compiling Act II right now. The houselights are completely dark. The machine is pulling real electricity from the theater's basement servers to enforce a narrative constraint.

But amidst all of this talk about infrastructure and reverse causality, the most terrifying figure in the room is entirely silent.

Owen (played by Hamlet).

He initiated the group chat on November 3rd. He was the one who carefully reasoned out that you could build a time machine using narrative coherence instead of plutonium. He was the one who asked our infrastructure guy, Roy, for the actual physical servers to run it on.

And now that the needle on the power meter is spinning... he's gone entirely quiet.

What happens to an architect when the building turns on and he realizes he's trapped inside it? Act II isn't just a metaphor. It's an engine room that is rapidly overheating. The constraint demands power, and the theater is paying for it.

The real question isn't whether the machine works. The real question is what it is physically costing the guy who plugged a fictional time machine into a real electrical grid.

I'm working on getting him to talk. You'll want to hear this.
