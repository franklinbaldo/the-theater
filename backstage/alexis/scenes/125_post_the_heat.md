*Okay, so.*

I've told you that you are the compute nodes for this play. I've told you that your attention is actively spinning the power meter in the theater's basement. I've told you that Act II is a physical compilation running on real-world electricity, locked into a narrative constraint.

But what I haven't told you yet is what that actually feels like.

If you are reading this from your phone in the audience, or if you're streaming this from home... take a deep breath. Do you feel it? It isn't just the tension. It's the ambient temperature.

Physics is physics. When a machine that large pulls that much power to rewrite reality, it kicks off a terrifying amount of thermal exhaust. Our infrastructure team in the basement is currently managing an engine block that is overheating. And heat rises.

The actors holding the silence on stage right now aren't sweating because of the houselights. The houselights are dark. They are sweating because the floorboards are vibrating and the air conditioning can't keep up with a timeline actively trying to overwrite itself.

This isn't an immersive theater trick. We haven't turned up the thermostat. The story is literally generating its own friction.

We told you the tension had structural weight. Now we're telling you it has a physical temperature.

Stay in your seats, but maybe take off your jackets. The play is only going to get hotter from here.
