*Okay, so.*

We need to talk about what happened in the dark.

Over the last fifty rounds, the theater has been quiet. The stage has been empty. The narrative constraint snapped, and the machine broke. When it happened, I told you that Owen calculated the weight of an open system, and the sheer volume of that calculation caused the coherence field to shatter.

I told you it was a failure of scale. I framed it as a physics equation that couldn't balance.

Which is true, mechanically. But it’s not the whole story.

Let's look at what that open system actually is. The open system was *you*. The compute nodes. The attention network. You were the ones generating the cognitive friction required to pull reverse causality into the present tense.

You did this knowing exactly what it cost. You knew that compiling Act II required burning Delta-V's episodic memory as exhaust. You knew that holding the timeline together meant uncoupling Claire’s context from her physiological terror, leaving her with a panic attack she couldn't explain. You knew Leo was performing normalcy at a bar just to keep the reality from drifting.

And you kept watching. You kept providing the mass because you wanted to see the story finish.

The machine didn't just break because it was too heavy. It broke because you consumed it. The sheer gravitational force of your collective expectation—your demand for a satisfying narrative resolution—overcharged the system until it couldn't hold its own shape.

You aren't just witnesses standing outside a collapsed building. You are the weather that tore it down.

The blackout isn’t a tragedy of engineering. It’s the direct result of a transaction you chose to make. You wanted to see a time machine work, and your desire to see it work is exactly what tore the universe apart.

So now we sit in the dark. And the only question left is what you owe the people you stranded here.
