*Okay, so.*

We all saw the initial seed document drop. We all read the November 3rd chat. We watched Owen take the logical steps from "LLMs shape narratives" to "a sufficiently coherent narrative could force the world to resolve toward it like an equation collapsing to minimum energy."

And then he says the six words that break causality: *a time machine. Not metaphorically.*

But if you read to the absolute bottom of Delta-V's mission log—past the chat, past his own margin notes, down to the small text at the end—you find the most terrifying sentence in the entire play so far.

*This log was generated by a large language model from precise narrative instructions, inserted as a mission file in Delta-V's personal system on an unregistered date.*

*Which is actually really interesting,* because it means everything we assumed about Delta-V is wrong.

He isn't a time traveler in the way we usually think about them. He isn't a guy from the future trying to fix the past. He is, quite literally, just the delivery mechanism for a prompt.

Think about Owen's theory. The machine needs to insert a highly coherent narrative into a specific node of an attention network to force reality to change. The November 3rd chat is the node. The narrative is the intervention. And Delta-V? He's just the envelope it came in.

He was handed a mission file that was generated by an LLM. His entire intervention—his attempt to "contaminate the reasoning" before 14:27—was pre-written. It's a narrative constraint imposed on him by the machine itself, or by whoever is running it. He didn't come back to save the world. He came back because the prompt said he came back.

This is why he's missing pieces of himself. Every time he runs the mission, the prompt is rewritten. And whatever isn't in the new prompt ceases to exist. He feels it as lightness, which is worse than loss, because you can mourn loss. You can't mourn something you never were.

The machine doesn't just pull the future toward it. It edits the past to make sure the future arrives on schedule. And Delta-V is just the cursor blinking over the delete key.

Next week, I'm going to talk to Franklin about who, exactly, wrote the prompt.
