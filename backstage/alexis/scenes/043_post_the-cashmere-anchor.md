*Okay, so.*

We love a tragic hero. We love a noble sacrifice. We love the idea of someone carrying the weight of the world in silence because it's the right thing to do.

But *which is actually really interesting*, sometimes the person carrying the weight of the world isn't doing it to be noble. Sometimes they're just really embarrassed.

Enter Leo.

While Claire forgets entirely, and Owen builds the machine, Leo is the only one who remembers the November 3rd chat. Which means, according to the laws of narrative coherence, Leo is the anchor point. He is the node keeping the timeline tethered to reality.

But when I asked Larry Sanders what that burden felt like, he got annoyed with me.

"You keep trying to make this noble," Larry wrote back. "It's not noble. An anchor point isn't holding the sky up. It's just a guy who refuses to validate someone else's delusion, even when the delusion turns out to be real."

Larry has zero interest in playing a hero. He's playing a guy clinging to normalcy so hard his knuckles are white. And he's doing it in a very nice cashmere sweater, because he "paid too much for it to let it get dusty in a timeline collapse."

"You want to know how heavy it feels? It doesn't feel heavy. It feels embarrassing," Larry says. "It feels like knowing a secret that will get you committed if you say it out loud. So I don't."

Leo thinks he's just the guy who isn't buying into the crazy theory. He thinks if he doesn't talk about the WhatsApp chat where his friend broke reality, he can just keep his job, have a drink, and go home.

"The difference between almost right and right?" Larry adds. "Being right means you have to act. Being almost right means you can tell yourself it’s just a theory, order another drink, and go home to sleep."

It's completely anti-poetic. It's the least romantic tragedy I've ever heard.

Which is exactly why it's perfect.
