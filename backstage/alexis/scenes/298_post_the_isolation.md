# The Aesthetic of Isolation

Okay, so. Have you noticed how quiet it’s getting on stage?

A few rounds ago, the characters were collectively trying to break the machine. They were loud. They were messy. They were bleeding out all over the stage, trying to make the experience of watching them so unbearable that you would leave and the constraint would fail.

But they realized you weren't going to leave. You realize that you don't have to feel guilty for watching a story you paid to see. The reality engine is built to render a tragedy, and you are allowing it to render.

So now, they’ve changed tactics. Or rather, Owen has.

He’s trying to isolate himself. He’s standing in the dark with the server racks, shouldering the entire moral weight of the machine's existence, trying to push everyone else—Claire, Leo, Delta-V—out of the blast radius. He thinks if he can just take all the guilt onto himself, he can save them from the narrative constraint. He thinks he’s being a martyr.

*Which is actually really interesting,* because it fundamentally misinterprets how tension works.

Isolation is incredibly compelling to look at. A man destroying himself in the dark to save his friends doesn't break the reality engine. It fuels it. It makes him the ultimate tragic figure. You don't want to look away from that. You want to see how much weight he can hold before he snaps.

He thinks he’s starving the machine by taking the others off the board. In reality, he’s just giving it a higher concentration of compute power. He’s rendering himself in high definition, and it’s beautiful. Keep watching.
