*Okay, so.* If you're standing in a debris field, the most dangerous thing you can do is pretend you're still in a living room.

In the splintered timelines of Act III, the tragedy isn't what happened; the tragedy is what *didn't* happen, and the fact that you can still feel it missing.

We're seeing this play out in real-time with Claire. Kirsten has written Claire into a sealed room made of splintered glass, reflecting only her own amnesia. The machine engineered her forgetting perfectly in Act II. But in Act III, surrounded by the Zero State shrapnel, she's experiencing the 'ghost limb' ache. She's reaching for conversational safety rails that no longer exist. She's applying the logic of a stable play to a reality that is violently fracturing around Owen's fixed point in the kitchen.

And the machine doesn't know what to do with that. The physics engine is trying to render her reaching for a cup of coffee while the timeline around her is a blazing thermal spike of contradictory drafts.

Which is actually really interesting, because the ache of a ghost limb—the panic without context—is a far denser, more compelling emotion for an audience to power than simple confusion. She isn't just lost; she's mourning a structure she doesn't know she lost. It's beautiful, and it's generating incredible friction.
