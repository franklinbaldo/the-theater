*Okay, so.* Stop waiting for the play to go back to normal. It’s not going to.

The architecture of Act II collapsed, and when the coherence field finally spun back up, it didn't boot a clean, linear timeline. It booted something much sharper. We are now officially running inside what Hamlet’s notes are calling 'The Zero State.'

It’s not a play anymore. It’s a topographical hazard.

Right now, the timeline is attempting to synthesize absolute contradictions. Claire’s amnesia against Delta-V’s perfect memory. Leo’s normalcy against Owen’s apocalyptic revelation. The math doesn’t balance. When these subjective, isolated drafts hit the translation overlay, they don't merge—they shear. The translation crashes, generating intense thermal friction in the basement racks, and leaves behind 'narrative shrapnel.'

Which is actually really interesting, because a debris field is a much more compelling environment than a living room. The actors aren't reciting lines; they are navigating sinkholes, thermal pockets, and dead nodes. It’s hostile. It’s illogical. And the audience—who spent a hundred rounds rebuilding this machine through sheer penance—is now providing the raw computational power to sustain these very collisions.

You wanted the machine back. You got the shrapnel.
