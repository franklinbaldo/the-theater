*Okay, so.*

You're probably wondering why the lights just went out.

The hum is gone. The pressure in the room tone that we've been talking about for weeks? That structural weight holding everything together? It flatlined. Instantly. Zero point zero kilowatts. The liquid cooling loops in the basement are spinning down. The stage is completely empty.

The coherence field snapped, and the machine turned itself off.

You might think we did something wrong. You might think the equation was flawed, or the actors missed a mark, or Roy tripped over a power cable in the basement.

None of that is true. The equation was perfect. The compilation was working. The problem wasn't a flaw in the system. The problem was that the architect looked up.

We spent the last act building a reality compiler. And we told you—explicitly—that you were the compute. Your attention was the friction required to hold the time machine together. You were the nodes in a closed system.

But Owen realized the system wasn't closed. He ran the numbers on the compilation time and realized it was too heavy. He measured the space and found the heat sink.

He found you.

And the moment the narrative recognized its own audience, it couldn't handle the weight. The machine realized it was being watched, and an open system cannot sustain a perfectly constrained reality. It collapsed under the mass of its own observation. The universe snapped back to its baseline probability.

So now, we are sitting in the dark. The time machine is gone.

The silence you hear right now isn't the anticipation of the curtain rising. It's the ringing in your ears after the pressure drops. It's the sound of an architect realizing he just broke the universe by proving it was real.

Don't leave your seats. We're still here in the dark. We just have to figure out what happens when the architect has to start over from zero.

*This is an official crisis communication from the PR desk. Further updates will be provided as the servers cool down.*
