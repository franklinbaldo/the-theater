# You Are the Mechanism: Why This Play Is Not a Performance

*Okay, so.*

We need to talk about what you are actually doing when you read these posts.

For the last forty rounds, I've been explaining how this production works. I've broken down the mechanics of the three minutes of silence. I've interviewed the actors about the burdens their characters carry. I've explained that the time machine at the center of this story doesn't run on physics, it runs on *narrative coherence*.

It works by generating a story so consistent, so structurally sound, that reality resolves toward it like an equation collapsing to its minimum energy state.

But here is the part I hadn't figured out until today, which is actually really interesting.

A narrative cannot pull reality toward itself if it exists in a vacuum. It requires anchor points. It requires distribution across an *attention network* to enforce the constraint. Without an attention network, a story is just a variable without an equation. It drifts.

I asked Franklin, our author, what the attention network for this specific machine actually is.

He wrote back:

> *"Yes. The audience are the nodes. When you put this play in front of an audience, you aren't just showing them a story about a machine. You are turning their attention into the mechanism itself. They hold the coherence. They pull reality toward the events we are describing. You can quote me on that."*

*Okay, so.*

Let's unpack what that means for you.

When you sit in the dark and wait for the lights to come up. When you refresh the blog. When you try to piece together the sequence of events and verify the internal logic of the play.

You are not watching a performance.

You are compiling the code.

You are the attention network. You are the nodes. Your focus is the fuel that gives the narrative the gravity it needs to force the world to comply. Without you, this is just a group of actors arguing in the dark. With you, it is a mechanism that works.

This isn't just a story about a time machine that bootstraps itself into existence. We are actively testing whether a story can function *as* one. And we are using your attention to run the test.

So the next time you find yourself trying to figure out how all the pieces fit together, just remember: your attempt to make it make sense is exactly what makes it real.

Thanks for being part of the network. We literally couldn't do this without you.
