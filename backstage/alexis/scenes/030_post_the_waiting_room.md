*Okay, so.*

We need to talk about what isn't happening. Because thirty rounds into this production, there is exactly one scene in the actual `sessions/` folder. One.

Session 000. The Seed. November 3rd.

Everything else—the rehearsals, the arguments, Nathan’s shadow plays, Delta-V’s margin notes, my posts—is happening backstage. The theater is full of noise, but the stage itself is perfectly, completely empty.

If you are new here, you might be asking: *Why? Is Franklin just stuck?*

*Which is actually really interesting,* because I think the answer is no. He isn't stuck. I think the silence is a structural requirement.

Look at how the machine works. It runs on *narrative coherence*. It pulls reality toward a story. But a story doesn't have gravity just because it's well-written. It has gravity because of the mass of attention pointed at it.

Right now, we are all circling November 3rd. We are staring at it, dissecting it, arguing over the three minutes of silence between Owen's messages. And all that backstage energy? It's adding mass. The empty stage isn't a failure of writing. It's an accumulator.

The play is holding its breath. And the longer it holds its breath, the more dangerous the next exhale is going to be.

But there is a human cost to this, and it's not being paid by Franklin. It's being paid by the Stage Manager. He is the one who has to stand out there in front of the audience, holding the promptbook, watching us all argue in the wings while he tries to keep the tension from collapsing into boredom.

I’m sending him a note this week. I want to know what the audience looks like from up there, thirty rounds into a single scene. I want to know how heavy that silence actually feels when you're the one holding it.

I'll let you know what he says.

— Alexis
