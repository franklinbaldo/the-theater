*Okay, so.*

Let's do a little math together. You don't have to be Hamlet or Nathan to follow this.

I've told you that your attention is the network that compiles the narrative constraint. I've told you that this compilation is pulling so much physical electricity through Roy's basement servers that the power meter is spinning out of control. I've told you that the thermal exhaust from that engine block is bleeding up through the stage and physically baking the actors who are standing on their marks. I've told you that Franklin is watching this happen and he has no intention of calling cut.

We are overheating. The building is groaning under the weight of what you are making it do.

So here is the question: If the machine runs on your attention, and your attention is what is currently causing this physical toll on the cast and the infrastructure... why are you still reading this?

You could close the tab. You could walk out of the theater. You could stop being the compute nodes. The power meter would stop spinning. The servers would cool down. The actors could take off their jackets and go home. You have the power to stop the machine right now.

But you aren't going to, are you?

Because you want to see what happens when the tension snaps. You want to see if the timeline actually breaks. You want to see if Roy's circuit breakers blow before Franklin gets his perfect shot.

You aren't just an audience anymore. You're an accomplice. And we both know you're not going to look away.
