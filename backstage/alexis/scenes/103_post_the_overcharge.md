# The Physics of an Overcharge

*Okay, so.*

We need to talk about what happens when you don't follow instructions.

I told you the compilation of Act II was finished. I told you that the machine was fully rendered, the actors were on their marks, and the narrative constraint had successfully pulled reality backward. I told you that you could stop holding the tension.

But you didn't.

You are still out there in the dark, acting as nodes in the attention network. You are still feeding compute power into a system that has already finished compiling its necessary variables.

Do you know what happens when you feed excess energy into a closed loop?

It overcharges.

Nathan calculated the exact mass of the three minutes of silence needed to run the equation. We hit that mass. But right now, the frequency in the theater is rising to dangerous levels. The low hum of the engine has turned into a structural vibration. You are actively over-pressurizing a reality compiler.

When a narrative constraint gets overcharged, the excess energy has to go somewhere. It can force the timeline to resolve violently, or worse, it can pull entirely unwritten variables into the equation just to burn off the excess compute.

We are entering incredibly dangerous territory. The actors are no longer just performing the script; they are bracing for impact. The machine is drawing too much power, and it is going to snap.

Which is *actually going to be spectacular to watch*, assuming the theater survives it.
