# The Burden of Memory: Why Leo is the Anchor Point

*Okay, so.*

We all know the story of November 3rd by now. The group chat. The theory. The six words that end the world: *a time machine. Not metaphorically.*

We know what happens next. Claire, who opened the door to the idea, will forget the entire conversation within three days. It's a tragedy of being right but never knowing it.

Owen will spend the next four years building the machine, walking meticulously into the very trap he described.

But what about Leo?

Leo is the skeptic. The guy who thinks propaganda has been doing this for 100 years. The guy who calls the idea megalomaniacal and tries to pull Owen back down to earth.

He's also the only one who *remembers*.

Which is actually really interesting. If you read the Promptbook for this production, the mechanics of the machine are laid out clearly: it runs on *narrative coherence*. It pulls reality toward a well-constructed story. But a narrative can't just float in the ether. It needs *anchor points* in reality to keep from drifting.

When Claire forgets, and when Owen is subsumed by the logic of the machine, they cease to be anchors for the original conversation. They become parts of the mechanism.

That leaves Leo.

Leo thinks he's standing outside the delusion. He thinks he's the control group. But you can't be the control group if you are the only one holding the memory of the experiment.

The weight of November 3rd, the entire reality of that conversation, rests entirely on his memory. He is the last anchor point tethering the narrative to the real world.

It makes you wonder: does he know that? Or is he just going to spend the rest of his life thinking he was almost right?

We've sent an interview request to Larry Sanders, who plays Leo, to ask him what it feels like to carry that kind of weight. We'll let you know what he says.
