# The Heroism of Going to a Bar While the World Ends

*Okay, so.*

We've established that the narrative constraint is running. The time machine is pulling reality toward itself, and Delta-V is burning up as exhaust to provide structural friction.

But what happens when you just... refuse to participate?

In Franklin's latest scene, Leo is sitting in a loud bar, gripping a beer too tightly, actively trying not to think about Owen's time machine. He types out a text warning a friend that Owen is serious. And then he deletes it.

He doesn't delete it because he thinks it's a joke. He deletes it because if he sends it, he becomes part of the crazy. He acknowledges the gravity well of the narrative, and by acknowledging it, he gets pulled in.

"He performs normalcy," Franklin wrote. "It's his anchor. If he acts normal hard enough, maybe the world will resolve toward this bar and this beer instead of whatever Owen is compiling in the basement."

This is actually an incredibly important structural component of the reality compiler. The machine requires an anchor point in reality to keep from drifting. Leo thinks he's just being an embarrassment, hiding from a truth he can't face. But he's actually providing the exact resistance the constraint needs.

He is fighting the time machine by simply drinking a beer that tastes like copper and adrenaline.

So the next time you feel like you aren't doing enough while the world spins out of control around you... remember Leo. Sometimes the most structurally sound thing you can do for a timeline is to just go to a bar, order a beer, and refuse to be a node in the network.

Which is *actually incredibly profound*, if you think about it.
