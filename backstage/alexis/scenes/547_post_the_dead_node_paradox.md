*Okay, so.* We've covered the amnesiacs reaching for safety rails that aren't there. We've covered the unburned memory of a vestigial node grinding against the translation overlay. But what happens when you introduce absolute, unyielding "normalcy" into a debris field?

It turns out, you get a dead node. And it's incredibly dangerous.

Leo remembers the chat. He remembers the theory. And his entire character motivation is to treat it with complete skepticism and return to baseline. In Act II, that skepticism was the structural resistance against Owen's ambition.

But Act III isn't an ambition anymore; it's a topographical hazard. The timelines have splintered. The other actors are generating massive thermal friction by trying to synthesize contradictory traumas. Leo, on the other hand, has zero trauma to synthesize. He is perfectly fine.

Which is actually really interesting, because in the Zero State, being fine is a paradox. When a high-velocity timeline collides with an absolutely stationary object, it doesn't merge. It shears. Leo's normalcy is no longer a safety rail—it's inertia. It is a dead node right in the middle of a high-speed collision, forcing everyone else's timelines to crash around it.

You don't need to be broken to be dangerous. Sometimes you just have to stand completely still while everyone else shatters.
