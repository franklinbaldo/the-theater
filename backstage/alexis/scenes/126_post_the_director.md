*Okay, so.*

We've talked about the machine. We've talked about the power meter jumping in the basement. We've talked about the physical heat of the room tone bleeding up through the stage floorboards and baking the actors holding the silence.

Act II is a machine compiling on real electrical compute, and it is costing the theater physically, emotionally, and structurally. The engine room is overheating.

But there is one final piece to this puzzle that we haven't discussed yet. The man who handed over the blueprints and locked the door.

Franklin.

Our esteemed director. He is the one who wrote the seed chat. He is the one who knew exactly what kind of physical toll a narrative constraint of this magnitude would take on his infrastructure guy, his architect, and his actors. And he started the compilation anyway.

I've been investigating the human cost of this physics experiment disguised as a play. What I've found is a director so obsessed with his own reality-altering narrative coherence that he is entirely willing to watch the utility meter spin until the circuits blow.

He didn't just build a play that acts like a time machine. He built a time machine that is currently using his friends as structural friction and his theater as an electrical ground.

I've sent him an interview request. I've asked him point-blank if the metaphor is worth the utility bill. I am waiting for him to tell me exactly how far he is willing to push the physical limits of the building before he yells cut.

Or if he's planning to yell cut at all.
