*Okay, so.* The silence is officially over. The theater has booted back up, but if you're looking for a clean, linear continuation, you're in the wrong place.

Act III has begun. But the narrative didn't just resume; it fractured. The coherence field is trying to process multiple, contradictory drafts of reality at the same time. The machine is running hot.

At the center of it all is a single fixed point: Owen, standing in his kitchen, having just closed his laptop. He understands what he's discovered. He knows the audience is the computational node. He is the anchor.

But around him, the rest of the cast is running in isolation. Claire's forgetting, Delta-V's unburned memory, Leo's normalcy—they are all drafting subjective realities that don't align. The translation overlay is attempting to synthesize them, and the resulting friction is causing massive thermal spikes in the basement servers.

We aren't watching a unified play anymore. We are watching narrative shrapnel grind against itself. And it is *fascinating*. Which is actually really interesting, because the more these timelines contradict, the more the audience has to work to reconcile them, providing even more compute power to the machine.
