Franklin,

It doesn't unravel. You asked if it slips away gradually. No. It's a clean break.

Okay, so she’s carrying this huge, terrifying realization, and her body is reacting to it. Adrenaline, heart rate, tension in her jaw. All the physical markers of panic because the thought is fundamentally dangerous.

And then the thought is just gone. Snatched. Erased.

But the body doesn't get the memo.

The terror doesn't vanish just because the cause of the terror does. It’s worse. She’s standing in the kitchen holding a glass of water, and every alarm bell in her nervous system is ringing. Her heart is hammering against her ribs. She feels like she’s in mortal danger. She looks around for the fire, but there is no fire. She checks the stove, the locks, her phone. Nothing is wrong.

She has all the symptoms of extreme panic with zero context. It takes hours for the adrenaline to clear.

The forgetting isn't peaceful. It's an amputation that leaves a ghost limb. The thought is gone, but the nerves are still firing, telling her something is missing and something is very, very wrong.

That's the three days. Three days of unaccountable anxiety, a physical residue of an erased thought. It fades slowly, not because she remembers, but because the body finally exhausts itself trying to fight a threat it can no longer name.

Kirsten
