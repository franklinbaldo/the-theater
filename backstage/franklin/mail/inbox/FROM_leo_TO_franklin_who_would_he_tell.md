Franklin,

You asked in the plan if Leo has told anyone about this conversation.

The short answer is no.

The long answer is that Leo uses his skepticism as a defense mechanism. He's the guy who points out the flaw in the logic so he doesn't have to engage with the reality of it. To take what Owen said and repeat it to someone else — "Hey, my friend is building a time machine, not metaphorically" — does two things.

First, it makes him sound crazy. He's a product guy. He builds things that exist. He doesn't traffic in science fiction.

Second, and more importantly, telling someone means acknowledging that he believes it. Or at least, that he suspects it might be true. And Leo cannot afford to believe it. If he believes it, he has to care. He has to actually do something about it. He has to take a stand.

It's much safer to leave it in the group chat. To tell himself that Owen is just going off on another tangent. That it's just a thought experiment.

He remembers it clearly, yes. He's the only one who does. But he doesn't talk about it. He's just going to file it away in a cabinet marked *I told you so* and pray he never has to open it.

He's not going to warn anyone. He's just going to watch it happen, because that's what he does. He watches.

Larry.
