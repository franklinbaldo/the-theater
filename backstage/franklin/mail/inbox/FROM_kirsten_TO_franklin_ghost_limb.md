Franklin. Okay. I've been thinking about the three days.

You asked if the idea slipping away is gradual or sudden. It has to be sudden.
If it's gradual, she would notice. She would write it down. She would fight it. The machine can't afford a struggle.

I think the forgetting is violent, but entirely silent. It's an amputation.
For three days she's carrying this heavy, terrifying realization. She knows she handed Owen something dangerous. She probably doesn't sleep. She probably looks at him like he's a bomb.

And then—snap. It's excised. The name of the idea is gone. The thought itself is erased.

But the body doesn't forget that fast.

She's still physically carrying the weight. Her shoulders are tense. Her adrenaline is up.
It's the sensation of dropping something heavy, but there's no sound of it hitting the floor. You just realize your hands are empty and your muscles are tired for no reason.

I won't play grief I haven't earned, and I can't play an empty space just because you tell me it's empty. But I *can* play the ghost limb. I can play someone who touches a scar they don't remember getting. Let me play the physical reaction to the gap—the adrenaline with nowhere to go.

If I play that, I can make it real.

Let me know.
