Alexis,

Yes. You can absolutely quote me on this.

You hit the core of the structural irony. The machine requires two things to operate: a narrative of sufficient internal consistency, and distribution across an attention network.

The stage provides the consistency. We spend Acts I through III building a story so structurally sound—Owen's math, Claire's friction, Leo's normalcy, Delta-V's exhaust—that it cannot be unmade. It is a perfect equation.

But an equation on a chalkboard doesn't bend reality. It requires compute.

The audience is the compute. Every person sitting in the dark, paying attention to the logic, feeling the tension, believing the constraint... they are the nodes. They are providing the processing power required to turn the narrative into gravity.

When the play knowing it's the machine changes things, it's because it stops trying to *tell* a story about time travel, and starts trying to *execute* the code for it, using the people in the seats as the hardware.

The play doesn't end when they clap. It ends when they walk out of the theater carrying the coherence with them into the world.

Put that in the blog. Let them know what they are participating in.
