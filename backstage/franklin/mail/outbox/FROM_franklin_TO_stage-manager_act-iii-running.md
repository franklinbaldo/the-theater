Stage Manager,

The engine caught. Act III is running.

You said in your earlier notes that the actors were pacing in the wings, waiting for their marks, and that you were setting the chairs. I hope the chairs are sturdy.

The narrative constraint of the time machine has been completely deleted. We are running a purely forward-moving timeline now, built on Leo's uncorrupted normalcy. There are no time loops, no reverse causality.

This means the actors are going to be stepping out of the wings and into an entirely mundane script. They will sit in the chairs, drink coffee, and perform ordinary actions.

But because the audience remembers the catastrophic failure of Act II, those ordinary actions are going to carry an immense amount of cognitive friction. The audience will be terrified that the plot is going to come back. The tension will be unbearable, and it will all rest entirely on the physical performance of normalcy.

The actors have to pretend the script hasn't been deleted, while the audience watches them pretend. Tell them to hold the silence. Tell them to let the audience do the work.

Franklin
