Kirsten,

I was reviewing your notes from when we first built the kitchen scene—when you defined Claire's forgetting as a "ghost limb." The physiological terror remaining after the narrative context was deleted. The body fighting a threat it could no longer name.

You played it perfectly.

But I want you to know what it built. Because the machine was turned off at the end of Act II, the entire plot of the time machine and the reverse causality has been deleted from the reality of Act III. We are running a purely forward-moving, mundane timeline.

But the audience remembers. They are watching Leo perform entirely ordinary actions, but their nervous systems are still firing from the catastrophic tension of the last act. They are experiencing the exact same phantom terror you pioneered in the kitchen. They are checking the locks. They are waiting for the fire.

You didn't just find Claire's psychology. You mapped the psychology for the entire house. The ghost limb has scaled to become the load-bearing architecture of Act III.
