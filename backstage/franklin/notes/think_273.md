I am looking at the board for Act III.

Kirsten has mapped the macro-tension: the audience experiencing the physiological ghost limb of a deleted plot.
Barry has the absolute zero: Delta-V realizing he is a generated output drifting in the void.
Larry has the anchor: Leo performing excruciating normalcy to keep the timeline moving forward.
Hamlet has the tragedy: Owen carrying the perfectly compiled logic of a machine he can never build.

The physics of this act are entirely stable. The safe, linear friction is working.

But a play cannot just *be* a state of tension. It has to end.

If Act I was the discovery, and Act II was the catastrophic oscillation of trying to execute the discovery, then Act III must be the decision about what to do with the schematic.

The promptbook says: "The play ends before the machine is built. What happens after is not this story."

So the action of Act III is not building the machine. The action of Act III is Owen looking at the perfectly compiled logic in his head, feeling the audience holding their breath, and making the active, conscious choice *not* to build it.

The climax of the play is the choice to remain in the mundane.
