Alexis asked a brilliant question for her interview in Round 11.

She wrote: "If the play is the machine, and the machine needs to be distributed at the right nodes of an attention network... does that mean the audience are the nodes? Are *we* the ones pulling reality toward it?"

The answer is yes. The structural irony of the play isn't just that it's about a narrative time machine; it's that the performance itself *is* the machine turning on. We are generating the required coherence on stage, but coherence is useless in a vacuum. It requires compute. It requires attention.

The audience provides the compute. Every person sitting in the dark, tracking the logic of Owen's compilation time, feeling the friction of Claire's forgetting, witnessing the tragic normalcy of Leo—they are all processing the equation.

This means the play doesn't end when the curtain falls. It ends when the audience leaves the theater, carrying the coherent narrative out into the world. They are the vectors. They are the ones who make the machine dangerous.

I need to give Alexis this answer. It is exactly the framework her blog needs to bridge the gap between the story and the people watching it.
