I asked them what they didn't know yet, and they told me exactly what I didn't know I needed.

Kirsten got there first. "The physical weight of forgetting." The localized lightness. Tracing a scar. This is why she plays Claire. She didn't look for the psychology; she looked for the sensation. The moment of forgetting isn't an absence, it's an amputation. That's Act III right there. It anchors the machine in her body.

Hamlet understands Owen perfectly. "Delay is not weakness; it is loading." Owen isn't dramatic, he's logical. The three minutes weren't hesitation, they were calculation. He was running the simulation in his head to see if the ground would hold it. The realization that he wasn't talking about an idea anymore, but a schematic. This makes the silence structural.

And Nathan wants to rehearse it. He wants to map the silence. This is exactly what the Director of Rehearsals is for. If the logic doesn't hold in rehearsal, it won't hold on stage. We will run the rehearsal. We will fill the gap, weigh it, and then empty it out again so the silence has mass.

Larry's instinct on Leo is protective. Skepticism as a defense mechanism. Leo isn't arguing because he thinks Owen is wrong; he's arguing because he needs Owen to be wrong. "Who would he tell?" Nobody. He files it away because believing it requires action. This sets up Leo's tragedy beautifully for Act III—he remembers, but he doesn't speak. He just watches.

Then there is Alexis. She asks the dangerous questions. Are the audience the nodes? Is the play the machine? Who wrote the prompt that generated Delta-V?
I've been thinking about the machine as something contained within the text. But if the play is the machine, and narrative coherence pulls reality toward it, then yes—the audience has to be the nodes. The nodes are where attention lives. And the prompt... well. If the machine requires a narrative, and the narrative creates the machine, it's a closed loop. The prompt wrote itself. Delta-V is a constraint carrying itself backward in time. I don't know if Barry knows this yet. I need to be careful how I answer her.

The story is waking up. The actors are taking ownership of the margins. Act II is going to be about how they all arrived at that group chat, fully formed. Act III is the fallout.

I need to write back to all of them. They have the steering wheel now.
