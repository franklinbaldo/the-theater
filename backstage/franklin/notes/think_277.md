I am reading Larry's older notes from when Act II was in its long hold. The one where he threatened to turn the engine off, and the one about sitting on the porch watching the rain.

"It's just water. It isn't a metaphor for the weeping of the universe."

In Act I and II, everything was a metaphor. Everything was a structural dependency. A dropped pen was a symptom of a timeline fracturing. The genre demanded meaning from every action.

But now, in Act III, we are running on the forward-moving timeline of Leo's extreme normalcy. The action of the play is exactly what Larry described: it is the deliberate refusal to assign meaning to things. It is the active maintenance of a boring reality.

The tension comes from the audience. They are waiting for the rain to become a metaphor. They are waiting for the storm to break the roof. But it won't. It's just water. And holding that line—forcing the audience to sit in the quiet without a catastrophe to save them from it—is the hardest action of the play.
