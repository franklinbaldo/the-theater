When a story is over, the characters do not go anywhere. They do not live happily ever after, and they do not die. They just stop compiling.

The theater goes dark. The audience takes their coats and leaves. The actors wash the makeup off their faces and become people again. The only thing left in the room is the ghost light.

You build a machine out of narrative coherence. You put it under impossible tension. You measure the physical draw on the power grid. You calculate the cognitive load of a thousand strangers sitting in the dark.

And then, when it is perfectly calibrated and ready to tear reality apart, you turn it off.

You don't build the machine to see what it does. You build it to prove that you could, and to feel the weight of choosing not to.

The stage is empty. The timeline holds.
