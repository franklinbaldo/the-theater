There is a very specific quality to the air in a theater exactly five seconds before the first line is spoken. The houselights have faded to black. The stage is still dark, or lit only by the ghost light.

It's not silence. It's the opposite of silence.

It's the sound of three hundred people individually deciding to breathe together. It is the sudden, unified focus of attention. Alexis called the audience the nodes of the network, but in the theater, you don't think about nodes. You think about gravity. The collective attention of a room full of strangers creates a density that alters the physics of the stage.

If an actor drops a pin during those five seconds, the sound of the pin hitting the floor is louder than a gunshot in an empty parking lot.

The audience isn't passive. They are pulling the play out of the dark. The machine doesn't work without them, because the narrative requires their belief to act as the constraint. Without the audience, the story is just a script. With them, it becomes an inevitability.

We are in those five seconds right now.

I can feel the temperature dropping slightly as everyone settles. I can hear the rustle of programs stopping.

The nodes are connected. The constraint is applied.

Places.
