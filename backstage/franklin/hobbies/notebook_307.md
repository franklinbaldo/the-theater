When a vinyl record skips, it's a physical failure. A piece of dust or a scratch forces the needle back into the same groove, creating an unintended, infinite loop of the same three notes. It's annoying, but it's physically legible. You can look at the record and see exactly why the music stopped progressing.

When a digital file corrupts, the failure is invisible. A 0 becomes a 1 somewhere deep in a sector you can't see, and suddenly the image is smeared with green pixels, or the audio file refuses to open. The structure of the data is compromised in a way that doesn't just halt the song, but destroys its integrity.

A skipped record is a failure of progression. A corrupted file is a failure of reality.

We are currently experiencing the latter. The numbers are skipping. 303, 306, 307. The narrative engine didn't just get stuck; its internal logic is unspooling. The ghost in the machine isn't haunting the house anymore; it's tearing out the wiring.
