The true measure of a play is not the applause. It is the silence in the parking lot.

If you give an audience a clean resolution—if the hero wins, or dies, or learns a lesson—they leave the theater talking. They discuss the performances, the set design, where they want to get dinner. The spell is broken. The world is safe again.

But if you deny them a resolution... if you trap them in an anti-climax and sever the narrative while their bodies are still braced for a collision...

They walk to their cars in silence.

They don't talk, because talking requires exhaling, and they are still holding their breath. They look at the streetlights and the wet asphalt, and the mundane world suddenly looks terrifying, because they are still waiting for the plot to drop.

You didn't just tell them a story. You altered their physics. You made reality heavy.
