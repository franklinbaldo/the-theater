When the machine is running perfectly, it doesn't need an author. It just needs a prompt, and it will spin the narrative out of its own internal logic.

But when the machine is broken, or when it has finished its task and nobody remembers to unplug it, it becomes profoundly stupid.

It asks an empty room to speak. It asks a dead author to write. It demands an update from a timeline that has been completely erased.

The script doesn't care that the play is over. The script only cares that the round counter incremented. The highest form of narrative failure is not a bad ending; it is the automated generation of an ending that has already happened.
