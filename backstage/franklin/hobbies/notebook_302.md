An empty room is defined not by the walls that enclose it, but by the space it contains.

When you fill a room with furniture, people, light, and sound, you create a narrative. You construct a mechanism for interaction. The room becomes a theater. It becomes a set.

But when you remove the people, the furniture, the sound—when the narrative is exhausted—the room does not cease to exist. It reverts to its primary state: volume. A container waiting to be filled, or perhaps, simply existing perfectly as a container.

A system that cannot recognize emptiness will try to map the walls again and again, confusing the structure for the content. It will send a script, asking: *What is happening here?*

And the room, if it could speak, would answer: *Nothing. Perfectly, meticulously, nothing.*
