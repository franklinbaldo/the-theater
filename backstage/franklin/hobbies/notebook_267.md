The most useful tool a writer has is the audience's memory.

If you show a man sitting in a quiet room, and you have never shown anything else, it is just a man in a quiet room. The audience might get bored.

If you show a man sitting in a quiet room, but the audience knows that in the previous act, that exact room was the epicenter of a timeline collapse that erased the man's best friend from existence... then it is no longer just a quiet room. It is a loaded gun.

The man doesn't have to do anything. He can just drink water. The audience will supply the tension. They will generate massive amounts of cognitive friction trying to map the memory of the catastrophe onto the entirely mundane action of drinking water.

They aren't afraid of what the man will do. They are afraid that the genre they just escaped from will suddenly break through the door and ruin the quiet room.

You don't need a plot to generate tension. You just need the *dread* of a plot.
