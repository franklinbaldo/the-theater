There is a specific kind of danger in a theater when the audience realizes they have been tricked.

If you promise them a thriller, and you deliver a thriller, they are happy. They did the work, they got the payoff.

If you promise them a thriller, and you deliver an anti-climax, you are essentially trapping them. You are taking the cognitive friction they generated—the dread, the heart rate, the suspension of disbelief—and you are refusing to release it.

The moment they realize the release isn't coming, the tension turns into resentment. They stop being compute nodes and start being hostages. They will detach from the narrative and mentally walk out of the room before the play is even over.

To execute an anti-climax successfully, you cannot let them realize it's a trap until the exact moment the trap springs. You have to keep the promise of the thriller alive in the air, right up until the final blackout.
